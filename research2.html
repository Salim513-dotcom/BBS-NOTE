What is a Research Problem?

A clear statement about an area of concern, a condition to be improved, or a difficulty to be eliminated that points to the need for meaningful understanding and deliberate investigation.

üîçFinding Your Research Problem

1 Management Problem

Simple Explanation: A real-world issue that organizations face and need solutions for.

Example: CG TV Sales Problem

Company noticed TV sales were dropping despite advertising - management needed to figure out why and fix it.

2 Research Problem

Simple Explanation: Turning the management problem into a specific question that research can answer.

Example Research Question:

"Does the new TV advertisement actually increase sales?"

3 Research Questions

Simple Explanation: Breaking the main research problem into smaller, specific questions.

Example Smaller Questions:

  • Can staff training improve sales?
  • Should we change the advertisement?
  • Would better product quality help?

üöóReal Example: Mustang Jeep

1

Management Problem

Low market share - not selling enough Jeeps

2

Research Problem

Can changing the Jeep's body and features help?

3

Research Question

Will better design increase Jeep sales?

4

Hypothesis

Improved design leads to higher Mustang Jeep sales

üîÑCreating Hypotheses

üí≠

Where Do Hypotheses Come From?

You can develop hypotheses by observing society, studying existing research, talking to experts, or examining data.

Hypothesis Development Process:

1

Observation

Notice something interesting in the world

2

Hypothesis

Make an educated guess about why it happens

3

Theory

Develop explanation if hypothesis is proven

üìãTypes of Hypotheses

1 Descriptive Hypothesis

Simple Explanation: Describes characteristics of a single variable.

Examples:

  • NABIL Bank shareholders prefer bonus dividends
  • Dharan-Dhankutta highway has high accident rates

2 Relational Hypothesis

Simple Explanation: Shows how two or more variables are connected.

Examples:

  • Higher income families spend more on recreation
  • More education increases political participation
  • Income growth expands product markets

3 Directional vs Non-directional

Directional: Predicts specific direction of relationship

Non-directional: Just says relationship exists without specifying direction

Directional Examples:

  • Younger workers are less motivated than older workers
  • More workload means less job satisfaction

Non-directional Examples:

  • Nepalese and Chinese work cultures differ
  • Education doesn't affect work commitment

4 Null vs Alternative

Null (H‚ÇÄ): Says "no relationship" between variables

Alternative (H‚ÇÅ): Says "relationship exists" between variables

Null Hypothesis Examples:

  • Training doesn't affect productivity
  • No gender difference in office work performance

Alternative Hypothesis Examples:

  • Training improves productivity
  • Gender affects performance under pressure

Two Ways to Think About Research

Deductive reasoning starts with general theories and tests them, while inductive reasoning starts with specific observations and builds theories.

‚¨áÔ∏èDeductive Reasoning

üîç

Top-Down Approach

Start with a general theory, develop specific hypothesis, collect data to test it, and draw conclusions about the theory.

Deductive Process:

1

Start with Theory

Begin with existing general knowledge

2

Make Prediction

Create specific hypothesis from theory

3

Test with Data

Collect information to check hypothesis

4

Draw Conclusion

Confirm, modify, or reject original theory

‚¨ÜÔ∏èInductive Reasoning

üí°

Bottom-Up Approach

Start with specific observations, identify patterns, develop hypothesis, and create new general theories.

Inductive Process:

1

Make Observations

Collect specific examples and data

2

Find Patterns

Look for trends in your observations

3

Develop Hypothesis

Create explanation for the patterns

4

Build Theory

Form general principles from findings
